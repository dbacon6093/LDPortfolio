Scenario-based Simulation for Internal Tech Support

AI Scenario-Based Learning Experience | Articulate Storyline 360

Project Overview

Internal support conversations often fail when the visible complaint is treated as the whole problem. A frustrated stakeholder may describe the issue as a broken system, an unhelpful process, or a failure by another team, but the real performance issue is often more complex.

This AI-powered simulation gives learners practice responding to a frustrated internal stakeholder, investigating a CRM workflow issue, and determining the most appropriate next step.

Learners step into the role of an internal tech support specialist responding to Marcus, a regional sales manager whose team's sales numbers are being negatively affected by a recent CRM workflow change. Through an open-response AI interaction, learners practice de-escalation, active listening, root-cause questioning, and deciding whether the issue requires troubleshooting, process clarification, or escalation.

Unlike a traditional branching scenario with fixed response choices, this experience allows learners to respond in their own words and receive an immediate score with targeted feedback based on the quality of their response.

Design Strategy

The experience is structured around an AI stakeholder interaction rather than a fixed multiple-choice path. Marcus, the frustrated sales manager, responds dynamically to the learner's input while staying anchored to the core scenario details.

The simulation is designed to reward several effective approaches, including:

  • Acknowledging the stakeholder's frustration
  • Asking when the issue started
  • Clarifying which part of the CRM workflow is causing friction
  • Connecting the issue to sales activity and data quality
  • Distinguishing between a technical malfunction and a workflow design issue
  • Requesting specific examples
  • Summarizing the issue accurately
  • Recommending escalation to the appropriate owner

This structure prevents the simulation from becoming a "guess the correct response" activity. Instead, learners can succeed through multiple realistic paths as long as they demonstrate effective support behaviors.

Performance Gap Addressed

Support employees can sometimes move too quickly toward a solution before fully understanding the problem. In high-pressure conversations, this can make stakeholders feel dismissed and may lead to incomplete or inappropriate support.

Common performance challenges include:

  • Responding defensively to frustration
  • Treating the issue as a basic technical problem too quickly
  • Offering generic troubleshooting before understanding the workflow
  • Missing the business impact behind the complaint
  • Failing to distinguish between user error, system behavior, process friction, and escalation needs
  • Promising a fix before enough information has been gathered
  • Ending the conversation without a clear next step

This simulation reframes support as a diagnostic conversation, not just a ticket-resolution task.

AI Feedback Design Decisions

The AI interaction was designed to give learners more flexibility than a traditional branching scenario while still maintaining instructional structure.

The scoring and feedback focus on whether the learner:

  • Acknowledges Marcus's frustration
  • Keeps the conversation professional
  • Asks focused diagnostic questions
  • Identifies the CRM workflow issue
  • Connects the issue to sales and data-quality impact
  • Avoids blame or defensiveness
  • Recognizes when escalation is appropriate
  • Communicates a clear next step

Feedback is immediate so learners can connect their response choices to the outcome of the conversation. The goal is not simply to tell learners whether they were "right" or "wrong," but to help them understand how their communication choices affected the quality of the support interaction.

Why This Project

I designed this simulation to demonstrate how AI-powered scenario practice can support realistic workplace skill development. The project reflects my approach to instructional design: creating practice opportunities that mirror real performance contexts, require learner judgment, and provide feedback that supports transfer.

This project also allowed me to move beyond fixed branching choices and explore a more flexible simulation model where learners can respond naturally. By designing the AI character behavior, learner goals, scoring criteria, and feedback structure, I focused on creating an experience that evaluates the quality of the learner's thinking, not just their ability to choose from predetermined options.
