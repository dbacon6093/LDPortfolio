Flagship Project

Navigating Difficult Conversations

Scenario-Based Learning Experience | Articulate Storyline 360

Workplace performance issues are often not caused by lack of effort, but by misaligned expectations, unclear communication, and delayed feedback. Many supervisors avoid addressing these problems early because they lack a structured approach to difficult conversations. This scenario-based learning experience helps managers practice responding in a realistic, low-risk environment before applying those skills on the job.

In this module, learners step into the role of Jordan, a manager preparing for a follow-up conversation with Alex, a new team member whose work is not meeting expectations despite strong effort and positive intent. Through branching decision points, learners experience how tone, timing, and clarity shape the outcome of the conversation.

Learning Goal

The goal of this experience is to help supervisors navigate difficult conversations in a way that clarifies expectations, maintains working relationships, and supports performance improvement.

Rather than presenting a checklist of correct responses, the scenario allows learners to practice making decisions in context and observe how those decisions influence both the conversation and its outcome.

Why a Scenario-Based Approach

Difficult conversations are interpersonal, dynamic, and emotionally charged. They cannot be mastered through static content alone.

A scenario-based format allows learners to:

  • test conversational strategies safely
  • see consequences of tone and framing choices
  • recover from missteps and adjust approach
  • recognize how small wording differences affect outcomes
  • practice aligning expectations collaboratively instead of defensively

This mirrors the kinds of decisions supervisors make in real workplace interactions.

Design Strategy

This experience was structured as a guided branching scenario with reconvergence points. Each decision moment represents a realistic managerial choice rather than an obvious correct answer.

Instead of rewarding only ideal responses, the design includes:

  • effective paths that build alignment
  • partial-success paths that resolve the issue but miss support opportunities
  • escalation paths that increase defensiveness or confusion
  • recovery opportunities that allow learners to adjust their approach

This reflects the reality that workplace conversations rarely succeed or fail in a single moment.

Instructional Framework

The module structure draws on principles from Gagné’s Events of Instruction to support learner engagement and transfer:

  • Context setting introduces the performance gap and relationship dynamics
  • Learner role framing establishes ownership of the conversation
  • Decision points provide active practice opportunities
  • Immediate feedback reinforces reasoning behind outcomes
  • Scenario consequences illustrate impact on both expectations and trust
  • Reflection prompts support application beyond the simulation

Together, these elements help move the learner from recognition to implementation.

Performance Gap Addressed

Many supervisors delay difficult conversations because they:

  • assume intent instead of clarifying expectations
  • soften language in ways that reduce clarity
  • address behavior without addressing workflow alignment
  • provide feedback without defining success criteria
  • treat conversations as corrective rather than collaborative

This module reframes difficult conversations as shared problem-solving interactions instead of disciplinary moments.

Feedback Design Decisions

Feedback appears at selected decision points rather than after every interaction. This allows the conversation to feel natural while still reinforcing learning at critical moments.

When feedback is provided, it focuses on:

  • the effect of tone on defensiveness
  • clarity of expectations
  • alignment between intent and outcome
  • opportunities to strengthen support structures

This keeps the experience reflective without interrupting flow.

Why This Project

I designed this module to demonstrate how scenario-based learning can support behavioral skill development in complex workplace situations. It reflects my approach to instructional design: combining realistic context, structured decision-making opportunities, and targeted feedback to help learners transfer strategies into practice.

The project also illustrates my workflow for planning branching interactions before development, ensuring each learner choice supports a clear instructional purpose.
