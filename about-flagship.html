Flagship Project

Navigating Difficult Conversations

Scenario-Based Learning Experience | Articulate Storyline 360

Project Overview

Workplace performance issues are often caused not by lack of effort, but by misaligned expectations, unclear communication, and delayed feedback. Many supervisors delay these conversations because they lack a structured approach for addressing concerns while maintaining working relationships.

This scenario-based learning experience allows supervisors to practice applying structured conversation strategies in a realistic, low-risk environment before using those strategies on the job.

Learners step into the role of Jordan, a manager preparing for a follow-up conversation with Alex, a new team member whose work is not meeting expectations despite strong effort and positive intent. Through branching decision points, learners analyze conversational cues, select responses, and observe how tone, timing, and clarity influence both alignment and trust.

Learning Goal

The goal of this experience is to help supervisors navigate difficult conversations in a way that clarifies expectations, maintains working relationships, and supports performance improvement.

Rather than presenting a checklist of correct responses, the scenario allows learners to practice making decisions in context and observe how those decisions influence both the conversation and its outcome.

Learning Objective

By the end of this scenario, learners will be able to apply structured conversation strategies to address a performance concern with a team member by clarifying expectations, maintaining working relationships, and identifying next steps for improvement.

Learners demonstrate this by interpreting contextual signals during the conversation, selecting responses at key decision points, and observing how those choices influence outcomes.

(Bloom’s Level: Apply, with embedded Analyze)

Why a Scenario-Based Approach

Difficult conversations are interpersonal, dynamic, and emotionally complex. They cannot be mastered through static instruction alone.

A branching scenario allows learners to:

  • test conversational strategies safely
  • evaluate the impact of tone and framing
  • recover from missteps and adjust approach
  • recognize how small wording differences affect outcomes
  • practice aligning expectations collaboratively instead of defensively

This mirrors the kinds of decisions supervisors make in real workplace interactions.

Design Strategy

The experience is structured as a guided branching scenario with reconvergence points. Each decision moment represents a realistic managerial choice rather than an obvious correct response.

The scenario includes:

  • effective paths that build alignment
  • partial-success paths that resolve the issue but miss support opportunities
  • escalation paths that increase defensiveness or confusion
  • recovery opportunities that allow learners to adjust their approach

This reflects the reality that workplace conversations rarely succeed or fail in a single moment.

Instructional Framework

The module structure draws on principles from Gagné’s Events of Instruction to support learner engagement and transfer:

  • 1. Gain Attention

    The experience opens with a short example of a stalled performance conversation, highlighting how unclear expectations and delayed communication create frustration for both manager and employee.

  • 2. Inform Learners of the Objective

    Learners are introduced to the goal of applying structured conversation strategies that clarify expectations, maintain working relationships, and support improvement.

  • 3. Stimulate Recall of Prior Knowledge

    Learners are prompted to consider common challenges they have experienced when preparing for or conducting performance conversations.

  • 4. Present the Content (Scenario Context)

    Background information establishes the relationship between Jordan and Alex and clarifies the performance gap that prompted the meeting.

  • 5. Provide Learning Guidance

    Learners receive practical framing strategies that support clarity, alignment, and collaborative tone before entering the scenario interaction.

  • 6. Elicit Performance

    Learners analyze conversational cues and select responses at branching decision points throughout the interaction.

  • 7. Provide Feedback

    Targeted feedback appears at selected moments to explain how wording choices influence defensiveness, clarity, expectations, and support structures.

  • 8. Assess Performance Through Consequences

    Scenario outcomes demonstrate how different conversational approaches affect both task alignment and working relationships.

  • 9 Enhance Retention and Transfer

    Reflection prompts encourage learners to identify how these strategies can be applied to conversations in their own supervisory contexts.

Together, these elements support movement from recognizing effective communication strategies to applying them in realistic workplace situations.

Performance Gap Addressed

Many supervisors delay difficult conversations because they:

  • assume intent instead of clarifying expectations
  • soften language in ways that reduce clarity
  • address behavior without addressing workflow alignment
  • provide feedback without defining success criteria
  • treat conversations as corrective rather than collaborative

This module reframes difficult conversations as shared problem-solving interactions instead of disciplinary moments.

Feedback Design Decisions

Feedback appears at selected decision points rather than after every interaction. This allows the conversation to feel natural while still reinforcing learning at critical moments.

When feedback is provided, it focuses on:

  • the effect of tone on defensiveness
  • clarity of expectations
  • alignment between intent and outcome
  • opportunities to strengthen support structures

This keeps the experience reflective without interrupting flow.

Why This Project

I designed this module to demonstrate how scenario-based learning can support behavioral skill development in complex workplace situations. It reflects my approach to instructional design: combining realistic context, structured decision-making opportunities, and targeted feedback to help learners transfer strategies into practice.

The project also illustrates my workflow for planning branching interactions before development, ensuring each learner choice supports a clear instructional purpose.
